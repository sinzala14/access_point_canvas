The framework

What is the Lean Canvas?

Adapted by Ash Maurya from Alexander Osterwalder's Business Model Canvas, the Lean Canvas is a one-page strategic blueprint purpose-built for founders who move fast and learn faster. It forces you to articulate your problem, solution, and market in language sharp enough to be stress-tested by reality.

Unlike a traditional business plan — which locks you into an assumption-heavy document for months — the Lean Canvas is designed to be wrong quickly, revised cheaply, and validated continuously.

"A problem well stated is a problem half solved. The Lean Canvas makes sure you actually state it."

Your roadmap

5 Phases to Launch

Check off each task as you go. Your canvas drives each decision.

1

Discover — Fill Your Canvas

Before writing code or renting office space, articulate every block of the Lean Canvas. Leave nothing blank. Blank = assumption = risk.

  • Write down the top 3 problems your target customer faces
  • Research how customers are solving those problems today
  • Draft one sentence: your Unique Value Proposition
  • Name your 3 customer segments in specific terms
  • Identify your Unfair Advantage honestly
2

Validate — Talk to Real People

Conduct at least 10 customer interviews before building anything. Your canvas is a set of guesses — interviews turn them into facts.

  • Recruit 10 people from your target customer segment
  • Run structured problem-interview sessions (no pitching)
  • Document verbatim quotes that confirm or refute each problem
  • Identify your 5 most excited early adopters
  • Update your canvas based on what you learned
3

Build — Minimum Viable Product

Build only the features that directly solve the #1 problem for your early adopters. Every feature not on your Solution block is scope creep.

  • Define your MVP scope in one paragraph
  • Set a hard build deadline (no longer than 8 weeks)
  • Choose your tech stack based on speed, not prestige
  • Ship a working product to at least 5 early adopters
  • Log every bug, complaint, and compliment
4

Measure — Track What Matters

Instrument your Key Metrics block. Vanity metrics (page views, Twitter followers) are distractions. Track what proves value delivery.

  • Set up analytics on your product (Mixpanel, PostHog, or Amplitude)
  • Define one North Star Metric for this stage
  • Build a weekly dashboard you actually look at
  • Set a retention target for week-1 and week-4
  • Run weekly reviews comparing metrics to your canvas assumptions
5

Iterate — Pivot or Persist

Every metric tells a story. If your metrics are flat, return to the canvas. Something in your problem, segment, or channel is wrong — find it.

  • Hold a monthly canvas review with your team
  • Document what you've invalidated and why
  • Decide: pivot one block, or stay the course?
  • Run another round of customer interviews
  • Update your canvas — version-control it like code

12 questions that cut through the noise

Validate Your Canvas

Answer each question honestly. Vague answers mean weak assumptions.

Problem

Can you describe the pain in the customer's own words, not yours?

Alternatives

Have you actually used the competitor or workaround your customer relies on?

Solution

Does your solution map 1-to-1 with every problem you listed?

UVP

If your UVP has the word "better," rewrite it. What specifically is better and by how much?

Concept

Can a stranger repeat your high-level concept back to you after hearing it once?

Unfair Advantage

Could a well-funded team replicate your advantage in six months? If yes, it's not an unfair advantage.

Segments

Have you named a person, not a category? "Freelance designers aged 25–35 in tier-2 cities" beats "creatives."

Early Adopters

Do you have a name and phone number for your first 10 users?

Channels

Have you tested each channel with real spend or effort — not just planned it?

Key Metrics

If your North Star Metric doubles, does revenue double? If not, pick a different metric.

Cost Structure

Do you know your monthly burn rate to the nearest $100?

Revenue

Has at least one person given you money — or committed to giving you money — for this product?
